Adinkra origins: math and science as tools in history research

< BACK | CONTINUE >
Description Original object Intermediate form Adinkra symbol and simulation

Math, science and computing can help us figure out where Adinkra come from.

Photos of water drops are often used to study the physics of fluids. Ghanaian artisans were also keen observers of fluid shapes; we can see a similar pattern in the "soul washer" badge that was worn by the Ghana priests who conducted water rituals for the king. This suggests an origin for the adinkrahene ("king of adinkra") symbol: ripples of power spread in all directions.

Spiritual temples in Ghana often used an open courtyard surrounded by roofed verandas. The few that remain today have wood inlays in the doors that appear to show this spirital power spreading like the adinkrahene symbol. The adinkra symbol at far right has been identified today as meaning "church," but it seems likely that it originally referred to this idea of the spirirtual force associated with the traditional shrines.

Many shapes created by living organisms can be modeled using the arc of a log spiral. Some have several full rotations, like the snail shell. Others are just a short arc, like the neck of a bird. The reason that adinkra symbols often use log spiral arcs is due to this important feature of biological growth. To learn more click here.
